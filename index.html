Series Concept

Most design-build firms lead with their projects. This series leads with their people — the engineers, project managers, and architects whose judgment is the actual product. No talking heads. No scripted delivery. Each person speaks off-camera while the work appears on screen.

The cinematographic standard matches the work itself: intentional, unhurried, precise. Every frame should look like it was chosen, not captured. The edit rhythm reflects a firm that takes its time because it can.

Series Standards
Voice-over
All VO recorded off-camera and laid over B-roll. No talent speaks to lens in any video. Record in a single session per person — multiple takes, select the most natural pass.
On screen
When talent appears in B-roll, they should be unaware of the camera. No eye contact with lens. No staged reactions. Candid behavior, not documentary performance.
Pacing
4–6 cuts per video. Minimum 2-second hold per shot. Lock VO before editing — cut picture to VO, never the reverse.
Homepage
Videos autoplay muted on the homepage. The visual sequence must communicate the discipline without audio. Review each video muted before final sign-off.

Cinematographic Standards

Every shot should be able to stand alone as a still. If it can't, it doesn't belong in the edit.

  • Cinema camera or full-frame mirrorless (Sony FX3, BMPCC 6K, or equivalent). No phone or consumer-grade capture.
  • Prime lenses strongly preferred — 24mm, 35mm, 50mm, 85mm. Zoom only for telephoto architecture shots.
  • Shoot wide open or near-wide: f/1.8–f/2.8. Subject-to-background separation should read clearly in every shot.
  • Frame rate: 24fps primary; 120fps selectively for slow motion (crane lift, material detail, gesture shot).
  • Shoot in LOG profile (S-Log3, BRAW, or equivalent). Grade in post — never bake-in a look on set.
  • Natural and available light only. No soft boxes, LED panels, or fill on B-roll. If the scene needs light, change the time of day.
  • Interiors: use window light and practical sources. Expose for the highlights; let shadows open naturally.
  • Exteriors: golden hour, blue hour, or overcast preferred. Avoid midday direct sun on faces and architectural surfaces.
  • Video 03 drone shot: interior lights on 20 minutes before arrival. Blue-hour window is 8–12 minutes post-sunset. Schedule accordingly — there is no second chance for this shot.
  • Movements should be motivated — they reveal something, not decorate. If the movement doesn't serve the shot, lock the camera down.
  • Acceptable: slow push-ins, arc reveals, deliberate pans, handheld that reads as intention rather than instability.
  • Gimbal: acceptable for walking follow shots (Video 01 bike arrival, Video 02 site walk). Not a substitute for composition.
  • Drone: smooth, unhurried. No aggressive banking. Reveal movements only — the camera finds the building, it doesn't perform around it.
  • Portrait format is not a crop of landscape — compose natively for 9:16. Subject placement, negative space, and leading lines must be designed for the vertical frame.
  • Depth is mandatory. Every shot should have foreground, subject, and background — or at minimum two of the three.
  • For people: avoid center-frame symmetry. Off-axis placement, partial framing, and back-of-head shots are all appropriate when they serve the moment.
  • Warm neutral palette — natural skin tones, architectural materials reading true, slight warmth in shadows.
  • Reference aesthetic: Iwan Baan's architectural photography. Real materials, real light, no digital gloss.
  • Consistent LUT applied across all three videos. Variance between videos should come from location and light, not grade inconsistency.
  • No teal-and-orange looks. No desaturation for "cinematic" effect. Grade to the face first, then the environment.
  • Voice-over recorded in a dry acoustic environment. No room reverb, no compression artifacts.
  • Ambient sound layered under VO — job site presence, wind, interior quiet — whichever is authentic to the scene.
  • No music. No score. No sound effects that didn't exist on location.
Transitions

The edit should feel authored, not filtered. These are the only transitions used in this series.

Instantaneous. One frame ends, the next begins. The contrast between shots does the work. Hold each shot long enough that the edit doesn't feel rushed. Cut on action or on a natural visual pause.

Two shots share a compositional element that bridges the cut. Video 01: Graham's drawing diagonal continues as the crane cable angle. Video 03: The Sacks ceiling line continues as the horizon in the drone pull-back.

A moving element in the outgoing shot echoes in the incoming shot. Video 01: The beam's upward motion cuts to the drone's upward pull in Video 03. Video 03: Drone's upward pull cuts to a downward look at the tile samples — direction reversal creates the transition.

VO from the next or previous shot crosses the picture edit point. Use at the voice handoff in Video 03 — the image can move before Hailey's voice begins. Keeps the pacing from feeling mechanical at the transition.

A single shot slows to near-stillness, creating emphasis without a cut. Best candidates: crane beam (V01), the gesture shot (V02), Sacks drone reveal (V03). One use per video — overuse eliminates the effect.

Avoid Dissolves, wipes, cross-fades, flash cuts, whip pans, speed ramps used decoratively, or any transition that sounds like a preset. The only fade allowed is the fade-to-black into the end card at :13.
Production Notes
Capture & Format
  • Shoot natively in 9:16 portrait wherever possible. Architectural exteriors and drone footage: shoot 4K landscape, crop in post — retain landscape masters for future use.
  • Cinema camera or full-frame mirrorless only. LOG profile. Grade in post.
  • 24fps primary; 120fps selectively for slow-motion moments (crane, gesture shot, drone reveal).
Voice-Over Recording
  • All VO off-camera. No on-camera delivery in any video in this series.
  • Record each person in a single session. Multiple takes — select the most conversational pass.
  • Video 03: record Kelsey and Hailey separately in the same session. Edit into one continuous track with a clean half-beat pause between voices.
  • Lock VO before editing begins. Cut picture to VO — never the reverse.
Graphics & Editing
  • 4–6 cuts per video. Minimum 2-second hold per shot.
  • Name callout: white text, lower left, :04–:06. No background box. 0.3s fade in, hold, 0.3s fade out.
  • No other text overlays or motion graphics during the clip body.
  • End card :13–:15: "The People Behind the Work · Longfellow Design Build."
  • Fade to black into end card — the only fade allowed in the edit.
Deliverables Per Video
  • Final 9:16 MP4, H.264, YouTube optimized.
  • Muted autoplay version for homepage embed.
  • Static thumbnail from each video's strongest frame — not the opening.
